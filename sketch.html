Discovering a Solution

At this point we had clear understanding of who and what we are designing for, as well as what type of features we wanted to incorporate in our solution. It came time to think about building our solution.

In class, our group discussed various types of solutions. We sketched three prototypes that use different methods to solve the problem. We each performed two user tests for each of the prototypes to gather feedback on which will be the most successful. We asked the user to complete certain tasks during the tests to help us find identify the strengths and weaknesses of our prototype.

1.Punishment system

This method creates a study session and punishes the user when he/she loses focus by trying to access some distracting program on the computer. We considered various punishments such as the computer shuts down or an annoying/unbearable video will play. We ended up implementing a complex code the user must enter by selecting it on the screen.

Interview Questions

  1. Select the programs you want to use. Firefox and sublime. What would you do next?
  2. These are the sites that are blocked. You want to be able to watch videos, what would you do?
  3. How long do you want to use this for?
  4. Your session has begun, the k at the top leads to your toolbar. Say you want to take a break and check facebook, what would you do?
  5. You want to proceed anyways, what would you do?
  6. Use the code, read out what you would do.
  7. How would you go back to your session?

2. Motivational system

This system sends an alert to let the user know when he/she can take a break. While on the break, it sends an alert to let the user know when to get back to work.

Interview Questions:

  1. How long do you want to study for?
  2. How long do you want to have breaks for?
  3. How often do you want to take a break?
  4. This is what it looks like when you are studying/taking break/alerted to get back to work

3. Time Management system

This creates a to-do list for the user and lets them set a length of time to work on each session.

Interview Questions:

  1. You need to complete two tasks, how would you go about adding tasks. You want to spend 25 minutes on each task and have two hours total to spend.
  2. You start task 1 and want to take a break, what would you do? What would you do when you want to go back to work?
  3. Time ran out and you are still working, what would you do next?
  4. You begin task 2 and you finish right a t 25 minutes. You finished your task, what would you do?
  5. The summary will tell you the total time you spent working versus your desired time, the time you spent studying and the time you spent working.

Results

I performed user tests with Lily Dwyer and Olga Kazakova. Both said the punishment system would be the most effective, but it was the system they both preferred the least. Our overall interview results reflected that people like creating a study session that keeps track of the time they work. They also liked the feature that keeps track of their planned session time versus how long the session actually lasted and how many breaks they took and how long they lasted. Even though it was not the most preferred system, almost everyone we interviewed claimed the punishment system would help them focus the most. Keeping the feedback in mind, we decided to go with that system but tweak it in a way that felt less invasive and included elements from the other prototypes that people liked.
